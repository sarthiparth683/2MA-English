  • Summary:-
  • In Book I, the author introduces the novel by discussing the nature of biography and history, setting the tone for a satirical exploration of virtue. We meet Joseph Andrews, the brother of Pamela Andrews from Fielding's earlier novel Pamela. Joseph is an innocent and virtuous young man working as a footman for Lady Booby, who falls in love with him and attempts to seduce him. However, Joseph remains steadfast in his virtue, resisting her advances. Embarrassed and frustrated by Joseph's refusal, Lady Booby dismisses him from her service. Joseph decides to leave London and return to his home village to be with his love, Fanny Goodwill. On his journey, he is robbed and beaten by highwaymen but is later found by Parson Adams, a kind-hearted and somewhat eccentric clergyman who helps him recover. Joseph and Parson Adams embark on their journey together, heading toward their village. Along the way, they take shelter at an inn where Joseph’s modesty is tested, but he remains virtuous. The two have a philosophical conversation on religion and morality as they continue their journey, encountering various characters, including a poor pedlar who shares his food with them. They meet Mr. Barnabas, a clergyman more concerned with his own comfort than helping others. Parson Adams attempts to sell his sermons to raise money for their journey, but he is unsuccessful. They eventually reach another inn, where Joseph is joyfully reunited with Fanny, who was traveling to meet him. However, the innkeeper’s wife accuses Fanny of stealing a necklace, but it is later revealed that the necklace belongs to Fanny. Parson Adams tries to mediate between the innkeeper and his wife but ends up getting into a fight. The travelers resume their journey, with Fanny now accompanying them.
  • In Book II, the narrator discusses the importance of virtue and the challenges of maintaining it in a corrupt society. The travelers arrive at a village where they are invited to stay at the house of Mr. Wilson, who shares his life story with them. Mr. Wilson recounts his youth, his love for his wife, and his struggles with poverty and vice. After leaving Mr. Wilson’s house, the group encounters a squire who tries to seduce Fanny, but Joseph and Parson Adams defend her. They reach another village where they are hosted by a kind gentleman who entertains them with stories. The gentleman shares the story of Leonora, a woman who suffered from unrequited love. After leaving the gentleman's house, the travelers continue their journey, facing more obstacles. Parson Adams and Joseph get into a fight with a group of people at an inn, but they manage to escape. Finally, the group reaches their home village, where Joseph and Fanny are joyfully welcomed. The narrator reflects on the nature of true happiness and the dangers of pursuing wealth and status. Lady Booby returns to the village and tries to separate Joseph and Fanny by accusing Fanny of being unworthy of Joseph. She schemes to prevent their marriage by raising doubts about Fanny's parentage, but Parson Adams tries to convince Lady Booby of Joseph and Fanny’s virtue, though she remains unconvinced.
  • In Book III, the narrator reflects on the nature of human ambition and the pursuit of wealth. Lady Booby continues to plot against Joseph and Fanny, attempting to use legal means to separate them. The travelers face additional challenges as they try to prove Fanny’s parentage and secure her right to marry Joseph. A surprising revelation about Fanny’s true parentage complicates matters further. Despite Lady Booby’s attempts to thwart the marriage, she is ultimately unsuccessful and is forced to give up her schemes. The novel concludes with the marriage of Joseph and Fanny, contrasting the happiness of the virtuous with the misery of the wicked.
