  • SUMMARY:-
  • "Moll Flanders" by Daniel Defoe is a novel rather than a play, so it doesn't have acts like a traditional play would. Instead, the book is structured as a continuous narrative, following the life of the protagonist, Moll Flanders, from her birth to old age. However, I can provide a summary of the novel by dividing it into key sections or "acts" to give you a sense of the major events in her life:
  • Act 1: Early Life and First Marriage Background: Moll Flanders is born in Newgate Prison, London, to a convicted felon. She is raised by a foster mother who teaches her how to be a domestic servant. First Romance: As a teenager, Moll is courted by two brothers in the household where she serves. She is seduced by the elder brother, who then arranges for her to marry his younger brother. Moll marries the younger brother but soon becomes a widow.
  • Act 2: Marriages and Downfall Second Marriage: Moll marries a prosperous draper, but he quickly squanders their money and flees to France to avoid debt, leaving Moll to fend for herself. Third Marriage: Moll marries again, this time to a wealthy plantation owner from Virginia. They live happily for some time, but when they return to England, she discovers that he is actually her half-brother. Horrified, they separate. Fourth Marriage: Moll marries a banker, who eventually dies, leaving her with a small inheritance. She loses most of it through bad investments.
  • Act 3: Life of Crime Descent into Crime: Penniless and desperate, Moll turns to a life of crime. She becomes a skilled thief, pickpocket, and con artist, using her beauty and cunning to deceive men and steal from them. Capture and Imprisonment: Moll's criminal activities eventually catch up with her, and she is arrested. She is sentenced to death but manages to get her sentence commuted to transportation to the American colonies.
  • Act 4: Redemption and Final Years Transportation to Virginia: Moll is transported to Virginia, where she is reunited with her son (from her marriage to her half-brother) and her previous husband, who has also been transported for his crimes. Repentance and Prosperity: Moll repents for her life of crime and, with her husband, lives a prosperous life in the colonies, growing old peacefully.
  • Conclusion: The novel ends with Moll and her husband returning to England after many years, wealthy and repentant, where Moll resolves to live the rest of her life in penitence. This structure highlights the rise, fall, and eventual redemption of Moll Flanders, portraying her as a complex character who navigates a challenging and often morally ambiguous life.
