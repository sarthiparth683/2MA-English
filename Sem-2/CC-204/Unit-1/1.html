  • Daniel Defoe: Moll Flanders

  • Published in 1722
  • Characters:-
  • Moll Flanders
  • First Husband - Robin
  • Second Husband - Draper(Tradesman)
  • Third Husband - Plantation Owner
  • Fourth Husband - James
  • Fifth Husband - Banker(Clerk)
  • Moll Flanders (Protagonist) Role: Moll is the central character and narrator of the novel. Born in Newgate Prison to a convicted mother, she goes through a life filled with marriages, crime, and misfortune. Her primary goal is survival and securing wealth, often through morally questionable means such as theft, manipulation, and deceit. She represents a strong, cunning, and resilient woman who constantly adapts to her circumstances. Characteristics: Moll is pragmatic, opportunistic, and resourceful. Her desires for upward mobility and financial security drive much of her actions. Though her choices are often criminal, she remains a sympathetic character due to the harsh conditions she faces. Her morality is flexible, shaped by survival rather than ideology.
  • Robin (First Husband) Role: Robin is Moll's first husband. He is the younger son of a wealthy family who falls in love with Moll despite his family’s disapproval. After their marriage, Moll lives a relatively happy life until Robin dies. Characteristics: Robin is portrayed as kind, loving, and sincere. However, his role in Moll's life is short-lived as he dies early, leaving Moll widowed and financially vulnerable.
  • The Draper (Tradesman) (Second Husband) Role: Moll’s second husband is a draper or tradesman. He is a man whose business begins to fail due to poor financial decisions, and he ends up in debtor’s prison. Characteristics: He is irresponsible and financially unstable. Moll leaves him because of his mounting debts and eventual imprisonment, symbolizing her pragmatic approach to relationships, where financial stability is more important than love.
  • The Plantation Owner (Third Husband) Role: Moll's third husband is a plantation owner in Virginia, where she discovers that he is actually her half-brother. Upon learning this, their marriage becomes impossible to continue, and Moll leaves him. Characteristics: He is a well-to-do and seemingly respectable man, but his role in the story becomes morally complex when it is revealed that he and Moll share the same mother, making their marriage incestuous. This situation highlights Moll's continued misfortune and the dark ironies of her life.
  • James (Lancashire Husband) (Fourth Husband) Role: James is a highwayman who marries Moll, believing she is wealthy, just as Moll marries him under the assumption he has money. When they both discover the truth, they amicably part ways. Characteristics: James is charming, adventurous, and roguish. Like Moll, he is motivated by financial gain, making them kindred spirits in a way. Despite their separation, they have a mutual respect for one another, and later they reunite.
  • The Banker (Clerk) (Fifth Husband) Role: Moll’s fifth and final husband is a banker’s clerk. He helps her after her failed marriages and criminal activities, and he turns out to be a decent and loyal partner, despite Moll’s past. Characteristics: He is compassionate, patient, and forgiving, offering Moll the stability she had sought throughout her life. Their relationship represents a turning point where Moll’s turbulent life begins to settle.
  • Newgate Prison (Setting and Symbol) Role: Newgate Prison in London plays a crucial role in Moll’s life as both the place of her birth and where she is imprisoned later in life for her crimes. It is a symbol of the cycles of crime, punishment, and redemption that shape Moll’s story. Characteristics: As a place, Newgate is harsh and brutal, reflecting the grim realities of the legal system and the consequences of a life of crime in Moll’s world.
  • The Gypsies Role: Early in Moll’s life, she is raised by a group of Gypsies after being abandoned. They are part of her early experiences with poverty and survival. Characteristics: They represent Moll's initial exposure to a life of hardship and the lower rungs of society. They instill in her the mindset that would later fuel her self-reliance and opportunism.
  • The Nurse Role: A caretaker who takes care of Moll as a child and is one of the few people in her early life who shows her kindness. Characteristics: The nurse is nurturing and serves as a mother figure to young Moll before Moll is left to fend for herself. She represents the fleeting presence of warmth in Moll’s difficult early years.
  • Elder Son Role: Moll has children from her various marriages, including an elder son from her union with the plantation owner (her half-brother). The relationship is distant due to Moll leaving him in Virginia. Characteristics: He is mostly a figure in Moll’s past, a part of the life she left behind when her marriage to the plantation owner fell apart.
  • Younger Son Role: Another son of Moll, though little is said about him. Moll’s children often serve as reminders of her various marriages and the consequences of her actions. Characteristics: Like her elder son, he is largely absent from Moll’s later life, signifying her transitory relationships and lack of deep familial connections.
  • Humphrey Role: Humphrey is the son Moll has with her third husband, the plantation owner (and her half-brother). He remains with his father when Moll leaves Virginia. Characteristics: Humphrey is a minor character, more important as a symbol of Moll’s separation from her family and the dark moral complexities of her life.
  • The Gentleman of Bath Role: This character is one of Moll’s lovers, with whom she has a long-term affair while living in Bath. Their relationship is based on mutual pleasure and financial gain. Characteristics: The Gentleman of Bath is wealthy, charming, and indulgent. He provides Moll with a comfortable lifestyle for a time, but their relationship lacks deep emotional connection.
  • Jamy Role: Jamy is one of Moll’s children, mentioned briefly in the novel. Like her other children, he is a reminder of the consequences of Moll’s transient relationships. Characteristics: Jamy’s role is minor, but his existence contributes to the recurring theme of Moll’s fragmented family life.
  • A Bank Clerk Role: This character is involved in one of Moll’s many financial dealings, possibly connected to her fifth husband. He represents the financial networks Moll navigates throughout her life. Characteristics: The bank clerk is a symbol of Moll’s interactions with the financial world, where she often resorts to manipulation and crime to gain advantage.
  • Themes Highlighted Through the Characters Survival and Morality: Moll’s flexible morality is a reflection of her need to survive in a harsh world where women had limited opportunities for advancement. Crime and Punishment: Many of the characters, especially Moll’s husbands and the people she encounters, deal with crime and its consequences. Moll herself is deeply entangled in criminal activities, leading to her eventual imprisonment in Newgate. Social Class and Wealth: Moll's various marriages and relationships reflect the rigid class structures of the time, and her constant striving for financial security underscores the social inequalities she faces.
  • Summary of Moll Flanders by Daniel Defoe:-
  • Early Life and Background Moll Flanders, born in Newgate Prison to a criminal mother, starts her life in poverty. Her mother is eventually transported to America, leaving Moll in the care of a foster family. Growing up, Moll dreams of becoming a "gentlewoman," someone who can live independently without working as a servant.
  • First Romantic Relationship As a young woman, Moll becomes involved with the two brothers of a wealthy family. The elder brother seduces her, but instead of marrying her, he convinces his younger brother to do so. Moll marries the younger brother and has two children, but when he dies young, she is left widowed and poor.
  • Second Marriage and Life of Crime After her first husband’s death, Moll quickly remarries a tradesman, but the relationship fails due to financial troubles. They separate, and Moll begins a new chapter of her life, falling into a series of relationships and crimes to survive.
  • Third Marriage – Incestuous Relationship Moll marries a man from Virginia and moves to the American colonies. They live together happily for a time, and she bears him children. However, Moll discovers that her husband is actually her half-brother, as their mother is the same. Horrified, she flees back to England, leaving her children behind.
  • Fourth and Fifth Marriages Returning to England, Moll marries a banker. However, this marriage is short-lived as her husband dies. Moll’s next marriage is to a highwayman, a criminal like herself, which reflects her deepening involvement in theft and crime.
  • Life as a Thief After her fifth marriage, Moll turns to a full life of crime. She becomes a highly successful thief and pickpocket, using her wit and charm to deceive and steal from others. Over time, she becomes infamous for her criminal activities and manages to avoid capture for many years.
  • Arrest and Imprisonment Eventually, Moll is caught and sentenced to death in Newgate Prison, the same place where she was born. However, she is able to manipulate the legal system and receives a reprieve from execution. Instead, she is sentenced to transportation to the American colonies.
  • Redemption and New Life in America In America, Moll is reunited with one of her former husbands, and together they begin a new life. Moll repents for her past crimes and, with her husband, becomes a successful landowner. They live out their days in peace and relative prosperity, finally escaping the cycle of crime that had defined Moll’s life. This novel showcases Moll’s moral ambiguity as she moves between different social classes, relationships, and acts of deception, but ultimately finds redemption.
