• The Duchess of Malfi, Written by:-John Webster

  • Written by:-John Webster
  • Written in 1612-13
  • Published in 1623
  • Characters:-
  • 1. The Duchess of Malfi Role: Protagonist of the play, the young widow of the Duke of Malfi. Characteristics: Strong-willed and independent. She defies societal norms by remarrying for love rather than political advantage. Courageous and compassionate, especially towards her husband and children. Tragic figure, as her pursuit of personal happiness leads to her demise at the hands of her brothers.
  • 2. Antonio Bologna Role: Steward of the Duchess’s household and her secret second husband. Characteristics: Loyal and devoted to the Duchess, he marries her in secret despite being of lower social rank. Honest, moral, and often a voice of reason in the play. Insecure about his social standing but genuinely loves the Duchess.
  • 3. Delio Role: Antonio’s loyal friend and confidant. Characteristics: Supportive and caring, helping Antonio in his efforts to protect the Duchess. Loyal but practical, Delio acts as an observer and commentator on the events. In the end, he tries to salvage something from the tragedy by looking after Antonio's son.
  • 4. Cariola Role: Maidservant to the Duchess and a confidant. Characteristics: Loyal and protective of the Duchess, assisting in the secret marriage and supporting her in difficult times. Shows fear and desperation during the Duchess’s imprisonment, and is ultimately a tragic figure as she is killed for her loyalty.
  • 5. The Cardinal Role: The Duchess’s elder brother, a corrupt churchman. Characteristics: Cynical, manipulative, and ruthless. He represents the corruption of the church and uses his position for personal gain. He has an affair with Julia, despite being a high-ranking church official. Shows coldness and cruelty in his plotting against the Duchess and his own sister’s downfall.
  • 6. Ferdinand Role: The Duchess’s twin brother, Duke of Calabria. Characteristics: Violent, unstable, and obsessive. He displays an incestuous fixation on his sister, which is a major driving force behind his actions. His mental state deteriorates throughout the play, culminating in madness. Cruel and sadistic, he plays a direct role in the Duchess’s imprisonment and death.
  • 7. Bosola Role: The spy and assassin employed by the Cardinal and Ferdinand. Characteristics: Cynical and disillusioned with society. He serves as a villain, but is also a tragic figure. Though initially motivated by greed and ambition, Bosola grows increasingly remorseful and conflicted about his role in the Duchess’s death. His moral ambiguity makes him one of the more complex characters, as he ultimately seeks redemption, but too late.
  • 8. Julia Role: The Cardinal’s mistress and later involved with Bosola. Characteristics: Passionate but manipulated, Julia is trapped in an affair with the Cardinal and serves as a pawn in the machinations of the male characters. She seeks to free herself from the Cardinal’s control but ultimately meets a tragic end.
  • SUMMARY:-
  • Here’s a summary of John Webster’s "The Duchess of Malfi" act by act:
  • Act 1 The play opens in the court of the Duke of Malfi. The Duchess, a young widow, lives with her two corrupt brothers: Ferdinand and the Cardinal. They are both highly protective and prohibit her from remarrying, fearing that a new marriage would diminish their control over her wealth. Despite this, the Duchess secretly falls in love with her steward, Antonio. Against her brothers' wishes, she privately marries Antonio, swearing him to secrecy. Bosola, a spy employed by Ferdinand, is placed in the Duchess's household to keep an eye on her actions.
  • Act 2 Time has passed, and the Duchess has had a child with Antonio, though they continue to keep their marriage a secret. Bosola grows suspicious of the Duchess when he notices her behavior during pregnancy. He eventually discovers the truth and sends a letter to Ferdinand informing him that the Duchess has given birth. Ferdinand, enraged by the news, begins plotting revenge against her.
  • Act 3 Ferdinand confronts the Duchess and accuses her of dishonoring their family by secretly marrying beneath her social rank. He threatens her, but she bravely defends her actions. The Duchess and Antonio decide to flee with their children, but Antonio is sent away for his safety. The Duchess, however, is captured by Ferdinand and Bosola. Ferdinand’s rage intensifies as he loses control over the situation.
  • Act 4 The Duchess is imprisoned in her own palace by Ferdinand, who subjects her to cruel psychological torture. He sends her wax figures of her dead husband and children, leading her to believe they have been murdered. Despite this torment, the Duchess remains strong and refuses to submit to her brothers' cruelty. Eventually, Ferdinand orders Bosola to strangle the Duchess. Bosola hesitates but ultimately follows through with the command. The Duchess is murdered, but her dignity and courage remain intact.
  • Act 5 Following the Duchess's death, Bosola regrets his actions and turns against Ferdinand and the Cardinal. Antonio, unaware of his wife's death, seeks reconciliation with the brothers. However, a series of tragic events ensue. The Cardinal murders his mistress, Julia, out of fear that she might expose his secrets. Bosola, seeking justice, accidentally kills Antonio in the process of trying to kill the Cardinal. In the final scene, Ferdinand, the Cardinal, and Bosola all die in a bloody confrontation, leaving the Duchess’s eldest son as the sole survivor to inherit the Malfi estate.
  • The play is a grim exploration of corruption, power, and the consequences of unchecked ambition. The Duchess’s tragic end is a result of the malicious and tyrannical forces that surround her, but her bravery and virtue remain intact throughout her suffering.
