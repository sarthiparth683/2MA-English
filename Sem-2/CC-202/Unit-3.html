• Ben Jonson: The Alchemist

  • Written in 1610
  • Published in 1612
  • Characters:-
  • Lovewit
  • Face - real name Jeremy
  • Subtle
  • Dol
  • Drugger
  • Dapper
  • Mammon
  • Ananias
  • Kastrill
  • Dame Pliant
  • SUMMARY:-
  • Act I Scene 1: The play begins in the house of Lovewit, a gentleman who has fled to the countryside to escape the plague in London. His servant, Face, has teamed up with Subtle, a conman pretending to be an alchemist, and Dol Common, their accomplice. They plan to use the house to swindle gullible people by promising them magical services. Scene 2: The first victim, Dapper, a lawyer’s clerk, arrives seeking a familiar spirit to bring him luck at gambling. Face and Subtle convince him that he has fairy blood and promise to summon the Queen of the Fairies for him. Scene 3: Drugger, a tobacconist, arrives wanting to know the best arrangement for his shop to bring in more business. Subtle gives him some nonsensical advice.
  • Act II Scene 1: Sir Epicure Mammon, a wealthy knight, arrives with his skeptical friend Surly. Mammon is obsessed with the idea of the Philosopher’s Stone, which Subtle promises to create, making Mammon immensely rich. Surly, however, doubts the authenticity of Subtle’s claims. Scene 2: Ananias, an Anabaptist, arrives seeking gold for his religious sect. Subtle promises him great wealth if he and his leader, Tribulation Wholesome, invest in the project. Scene 3: Dapper returns and is prepared for his encounter with the Queen of the Fairies. Dol distracts him by pretending to be possessed by a spirit.
  • Act III Scene 1: Kastril, a quarrelsome young man, arrives wanting to learn how to argue and fight better. His sister, Dame Pliant, a wealthy young widow, comes with him. Subtle and Face both plot to marry her to gain her wealth. Scene 2: Mammon continues to be swindled, imagining the lavish lifestyle he will lead once he possesses the Philosopher’s Stone. Scene 3: Tribulation and Ananias return, demanding more results. Subtle and Face keep them at bay with more promises.
  • Act IV Scene 1: The various plots begin to intersect and unravel. Surly returns, disguised as a Spanish don, and attempts to expose the con. He tries to woo Dame Pliant for himself, but Face intervenes. Scene 2: Mammon’s greed leads him to be tricked into giving away his wealth. Dapper is bound and gagged, hidden away until the cons are over. Scene 3: Lovewit unexpectedly returns, and Face scrambles to cover up the operation. The tricksters’ various victims start to converge on the house, seeking their promised rewards.
  • Act V Scene 1: Lovewit is confronted by the angry victims. Face, now revealed to be Lovewit’s servant, Jeremy, concocts a story to explain the presence of all the people. Scene 2: Lovewit takes control, pacifying the victims and deciding to marry Dame Pliant himself. He agrees to cover for Jeremy, who escapes punishment. Scene 3: The play ends with Subtle and Dol fleeing, leaving Jeremy to continue his service to Lovewit. The victims realize they have been duped, but there is little they can do. “The Alchemist” is a satirical comedy that exposes human greed and gullibility, masterfully weaving together multiple plots and characters in a dynamic and humorous narrative.
