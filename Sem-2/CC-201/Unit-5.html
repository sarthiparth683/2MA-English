  • Dylan Thomas: Fern Hill

  • Here's a full summary of Dylan Thomas's poem "Fern Hill":
  • The Poem's Landscape The poem paints a vivid picture of Fern Hill, a farm where the speaker spent his childhood summers. Lush greenery, apple trees, and a starry night sky create a sense of idyllic beauty and harmony with nature.
  • Childhood Innocence The speaker reminisces about his youth, using bright and carefree language. He describes himself as "young and easy" under the apple boughs, completely happy and carefree like the green grass. Time seems endless, and the days are filled with endless joy.
  • A Deeper Look The middle section expands on this idyllic scene. The poem's rhythm becomes lilting, mirroring the carefree nature of the speaker's memories. Rich metaphors compare the farm to a paradise, with allusions to the Garden of Eden. The speaker describes himself as part of the natural world, climbing trees, chasing after cows, and swimming in the brook.
  • A Shift in Tone The poem takes a subtle turn towards the end. A hint of melancholy creeps in as the speaker acknowledges the passage of time. He realizes that his childhood summers at Fern Hill won't last forever. The line "Time held me green and dying" suggests a growing awareness of mortality and the inevitable loss of innocence.
  • The Power of Memory Despite the approaching adulthood, the speaker finds solace in the act of remembering. He vows to "eat the sunlight whole" and "praise cows leaping over fences." By capturing these memories in words, he hopes to preserve a piece of his childhood joy even as time marches on.
  • Themes "Fern Hill" explores themes of: Childhood Innocence: The poem celebrates the carefree happiness and wonder of childhood. Nature: The natural world at Fern Hill is a source of beauty, joy, and connection for the speaker. Loss of Innocence: The poem acknowledges the bittersweet reality of growing up and losing the simplicity of childhood. The Power of Memory: The act of remembering allows the speaker to preserve a part of his past.
  • Overall "Fern Hill" is a beautiful and nostalgic poem that captures the essence of childhood summers spent in nature. It's a celebration of innocence laced with a touch of melancholy about the passage of time.

  • W.H. Auden: The Unknown Citizen

  • W.H. Auden's poem "The Unknown Citizen" takes the form of a eulogy for a deceased citizen, but with a twist. It's not a heartfelt tribute, but rather a cold, clinical report, likely generated by a future government agency.
  • The poem details the life of this unknown citizen entirely through statistics and records. We learn about his voting record, consumption habits, family life, and even his leisure activities. However, none of these details reveal anything about the man himself, his thoughts, feelings, or individuality.
  • The speaker, the government agency, portrays the unknown citizen as the ideal model. He consumed the right products, followed the rules, and caused no trouble. There's a dark irony here. While the report celebrates his conformity, it also highlights the complete lack of individuality and freedom.
  • The poem's final lines are particularly striking. The speaker ponders whether the unknown citizen was "free" or "happy," but then dismisses these questions. The bureaucratic system the poem represents cannot comprehend such subjective concepts.
  • "The Unknown Citizen" is a satire that criticizes the dangers of excessive conformity and government control. It warns against a future where people are reduced to mere statistics, valued only for their obedience and contribution to a soulless society.

  • W.H. Auden: The Shield of Achilles

  • W.H. Auden's "The Shield of Achilles" W.H. Auden's "The Shield of Achilles" is a powerful reimagining of the classic Greek myth. The poem centers around the creation of Achilles' shield by the god Hephaestus, as requested by Achilles' mother, Thetis. However, instead of depicting the traditional scenes of heroic valor and idyllic peace, Auden presents a stark and bleak portrayal of war, dehumanization, and the futility of human endeavor.
  • The poem is divided into stanzas, with each stanza beginning with the line "She looked over his shoulder." This repetition emphasizes Thetis's growing disappointment as she observes the scenes Hephaestus is forging onto the shield. She expects to see images of beauty, peace, and civilization, reminiscent of the idyllic world often associated with Greek mythology.
  • Instead, Thetis witnesses a grim and desolate landscape. The shield depicts a barren plain, devoid of life, where a vast army of faceless soldiers prepares for battle. There is no sense of heroism or glory, only the cold, calculated efficiency of war. Auden employs a detached, almost statistical tone to describe the soldiers and their actions, further emphasizing the dehumanizing effects of conflict.
  • The poem also explores the themes of injustice and the erosion of human values in the face of war. The shield portrays a world where reason and morality are replaced by blind obedience and a relentless pursuit of power. The scenes depicted on the shield offer a chilling critique of the destructive potential of human society.
  • Ultimately, "The Shield of Achilles" is a powerful anti-war poem that challenges the traditional heroic narrative. Auden's bleak vision of the world serves as a stark reminder of the human cost of conflict and the importance of preserving our humanity in the face of adversity.
