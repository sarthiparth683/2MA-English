  • Fern Hill

  • Wirtten by:- Dylan Thomas
  • Written in 1945
  • Published in 1946
  • Summary:-
  • Stanza 1: The speaker reflects on the carefree and innocent days of his childhood when he lived on a farm (Fern Hill). He describes how he was full of joy, feeling young and connected to nature. The vibrant imagery of green fields and the sun suggests a sense of harmony and timelessness. He personifies time as something that allowed him freedom, where he was "prince of the apple towns"—a metaphor for the playful and imaginative world of youth.
  • Stanza 2: This stanza continues the theme of innocence and freedom, as the speaker recalls running around the farm, unburdened by worries. He uses language associated with pastoral beauty, referencing birds and animals. The landscape seems enchanted, and time is still seen as a friendly presence. The speaker's connection to the natural world is strong, and there is a sense of being in sync with the rhythms of nature, unaware of the passage of time.
  • Stanza 3: Here, the speaker begins to hint at the passage of time, although he still seems unaware of it during his childhood. The images of light and movement—such as the farm waking up and the skies being filled with birds—suggest the continuity of life. The speaker’s joy in his youthful surroundings is evident, but there is an underlying suggestion that this innocence is not permanent.
  • Stanza 4: In this stanza, the imagery becomes more reflective and symbolic. The speaker recalls his childhood as if it were a kind of Eden, a perfect paradise before the knowledge of adulthood. The "simple stars" and "spells of sand" evoke a magical sense of time, but there is also the suggestion that time is inevitable and always present. The speaker now seems to understand that time was always there, watching him even in his moments of carefree joy.
  • Stanza 5: The tone begins to shift as the speaker recognizes the reality of time. He acknowledges that while he was young, time was still passing, and this realization brings a sense of loss. He uses the metaphor of time as both kind and cruel—allowing him the freedom to enjoy his youth but also quietly guiding him toward adulthood and the loss of innocence.
  • Stanza 6: The poem concludes with the speaker reflecting on the inevitability of time. He is no longer in the paradise of his childhood; the carefree days of youth have passed. Time, once a friend, has become a force that leads to change, aging, and ultimately death. The speaker recognizes that, despite the joy and beauty of his youth, time has always been in control. The imagery of "dying" and "chains" suggests that the speaker feels trapped by time, but the poem still holds a reverence for the beauty of life.
  • Overall Themes: Fern Hill explores themes of innocence, the passage of time, and the inevitable loss of youth. The poem is nostalgic and reflects on the fleeting nature of childhood. The speaker’s shifting understanding of time—from a friendly, carefree force to something that constrains and ages—is central to the poem's emotional impact. The pastoral imagery and rich, lyrical language capture the beauty and transience of life.
