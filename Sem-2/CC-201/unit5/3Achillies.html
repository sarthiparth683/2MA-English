  • Summary of "The Shield of Achilles" by W.H. Auden
  • W.H. Auden's poem "The Shield of Achilles" contrasts the ancient world of myth with the harsh realities of the modern world. The poem is structured in two alternating perspectives: the mythological scene of Thetis looking at the shield and the contemporary vision of a barren, desolate landscape.
  • In the first part of the poem, Thetis, the mother of Achilles, expects to see scenes of heroism, beauty, and nobility depicted on her son's shield. She imagines glorious images of peace, prosperity, and honor, typical of the classical age. These are the kinds of scenes that Homer described in his epic, where the shield was a symbol of heroic ideals and the beauty of war.
  • However, what she actually sees on the shield is something entirely different. Instead of the expected pastoral scenes, Thetis sees a bleak, modern landscape. The shield shows an image of a desolate plain, where a crowd of faceless people are gathered, all under the watchful eyes of a tyrant. The atmosphere is one of oppression, cruelty, and despair. This contrast serves to highlight the difference between the idealized vision of the ancient world and the grim reality of the modern one.
  • The poem then shifts to describe a scene of violent and meaningless warfare. The soldiers are depicted as robotic and dehumanized, marching without purpose or glory. There is no honor or heroism in this battle, only death and destruction. This reflects Auden’s critique of modern warfare, which he sees as brutal and devoid of any noble purpose.
  • In the final part, the poem returns to Thetis, who is dismayed by the scenes on the shield. She realizes that the world her son will face is not one of heroism and honor, but one of suffering and futility. The shield, rather than being a symbol of protection and heroism, becomes a representation of the harsh realities of the modern world, where ideals have been replaced by brutality and despair.
  • Overall, "The Shield of Achilles" is a powerful commentary on the disillusionment of the modern age, contrasting the ideals of the past with the grim realities of the present. Auden uses the imagery of the shield to explore themes of war, power, and the loss of innocence.
