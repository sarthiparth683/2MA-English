• 3.1-G.M. Hopkins:- God's Grandeur

  • Written in 1877
  • Published in 1889
  • Main theme - God,Nature, and Man
  • Summary :-
  • Stanza 1: Hopkins opens the poem by marveling at the majesty and power of God, which he compares to a shining, electrifying force—“The world is charged with the grandeur of God.” He suggests that this divine force is both like a bright light and like oil pressed from olives, which bursts forth under pressure. Despite this grandeur, Hopkins expresses sorrow and frustration over how humans seem blind to God's glory. They live without realizing the divine presence, constantly exploiting and degrading nature through their daily activities. "Generations have trod, have trod, have trod" – humanity repeatedly abuses nature. Hopkins emphasizes the negative impact of industrialization—through human effort, the earth is being spoiled and “smeared with toil,” and the spiritual connection to nature seems lost.
  • Stanza 2: Despite the damage humans cause, Hopkins finds hope in the enduring power of nature and the divine spirit. He believes that nature is resilient, always capable of renewing itself, and cannot be completely destroyed by human actions. There is a continual rebirth in nature—“And for all this, nature is never spent.” "The Holy Ghost over the bent world broods" – the final image is one of protection and care. Hopkins envisions the Holy Spirit as a bird, like a mother hen, sheltering the world under its wings and ensuring its safety and renewal. The poem ends with a sense of comfort and optimism. No matter what humans do, God's grandeur continues to shine, and nature, under the watchful eye of the Holy Spirit, will always rejuvenate itself.
