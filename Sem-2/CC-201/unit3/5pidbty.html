• 3.2-G.M. Hopkins:Pied Beauty

  • Written in :- 1877
  • Published in : - 1918
  • "Pied Beauty" by Gerard Manley Hopkins is a hymn of praise to God for the variety and beauty of the natural world. Hopkins uses the word "pied" to refer to things that are variegated or multi-colored, appreciating God's creation for its diversity. Here's a stanza-wise summary of the poem:
  • First Stanza (Lines 1-6): Hopkins begins by praising God for the beauty of things that are "pied" or variegatedâ€”those with contrasting colors or patterns. He highlights specific examples of these, such as the skies that are "couple-colour'd" (having different shades or colors), the spots on a trout, and the wings of a finch. He also mentions the contrast of colors seen in landscapes, fields, and farm animals. The essence of the stanza is to show how things with variety and imperfection still reflect God's perfect creation.
  • Second Stanza (Lines 7-11): The second stanza shifts slightly in focus. Here, Hopkins acknowledges that all of these varied and unique things are the product of God's creation. He praises God for His ability to create things that are "fickle" or changeable, "freckled" or spotted, and for the existence of contrasts like swift and slow, sweet and sour, and bright and dim. The stanza emphasizes that even opposites and contrasts contribute to the beauty and harmony of the world.
  • Final Line (12): Hopkins concludes with the phrase, "He fathers-forth whose beauty is past change: Praise Him." This final line glorifies God as the unchanging source of all this change and variety. Despite the diversity and contrasts in the world, God's beauty remains eternal and unaltered. In essence, "Pied Beauty" is a poem of appreciation for the diversity and complexity in nature, recognizing the divine hand behind all things that are seemingly imperfect or unusual.
