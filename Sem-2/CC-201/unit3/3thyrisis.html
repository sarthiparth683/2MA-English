• 2.2 Matthew Arnold: Thyrsis

  • Publilshed in 1866
  • Elegy - A poem or song that express sadness, especially for somebody who has died.
  • Summary:-
  • Loss and Memory in the Oxfordshire Countryside "Thyrsis" is a poem written by Matthew Arnold in 1866 as a tribute to his deceased friend, the poet Arthur Hugh Clough. Written in the form of a pastoral elegy, the poem uses the idyllic setting of the Oxfordshire countryside to explore themes of loss, friendship, and the search for meaning.
  • The Framework: A Pastoral Elegy The poem draws heavily on the classical Greek and Roman pastoral tradition. Arnold refers to himself as Corydon, a shepherd, and Clough as Thyrsis, another shepherd figure from mythology. However, unlike traditional pastoral poems that celebrate a carefree rural life, "Thyrsis" uses the pastoral setting to lament loss and grapple with complex emotions.
  • The Journey: Reflecting on Friendship and Change The poem opens with the speaker (Arnold) revisiting the Oxfordshire countryside for the first time in years. This journey becomes a walk down memory lane, prompting him to recall his deep friendship with Clough during their time at Oxford. He reflects on their shared youthful idealism and intellectual pursuits.
  • Loss and Lamentation As the speaker delves deeper into his memories, the poem takes a melancholic turn. He mourns Clough's death and the loss of their shared past. The once vibrant and idyllic landscape now seems tinged with sadness. He observes the changes that time has wrought on the countryside, mirroring the changes within himself.
  • Beyond Loss: A Search for Meaning Despite the pervasive sense of loss, the poem doesn't end on a completely bleak note. The speaker finds solace in nature and the enduring power of memory. He acknowledges that their youthful ideals may be gone, but their spirit and the values they shared can still provide guidance in a world that seems increasingly uncertain and disillusioning.
  • Themes Explored in "Thyrsis" Loss: The central theme of the poem is loss, encompassing the death of Clough, the loss of innocence and youthful idealism, and the sense of a changing world. Friendship: The poem celebrates the deep bond between the speaker and Clough, highlighting the importance of friendship and shared experiences. Memory: Memory plays a crucial role in the poem, allowing the speaker to revisit his past and find solace in the face of loss. Nature: The natural world of the Oxfordshire countryside serves as a backdrop for the speaker's emotional journey, offering both beauty and a sense of permanence. Disillusionment: The poem reflects a sense of disillusionment with the modern world, contrasting it with the lost innocence and ideals of youth.
