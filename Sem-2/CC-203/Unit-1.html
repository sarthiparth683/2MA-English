    • Summary of John Galsworthy's Play "Justice" (1910)
    • Act I: The play opens in the office of a solicitor, where a young clerk named William Falder is accused of altering a check. Falder is in love with Ruth Honeywill, a married woman in an abusive relationship, and he altered the check to help her escape her husband. The lawyer, Mr. James How, discovers the forgery and decides to press charges. Falder is arrested, despite his emotional plea and his good character.
    • Act II: The second act takes place in the courtroom. Falder’s trial begins, and the defense attorney, Harold Cleaver, tries to convince the jury that Falder’s crime was committed out of desperation to help Ruth. The prosecution, however, emphasizes that altering the check is a serious offense. Ruth's testimony highlights her predicament, but the jury is unsympathetic. Falder is found guilty and sentenced to three years of penal servitude.
    • Act III: The third act takes place in prison. Falder struggles with the harsh conditions of his imprisonment. He feels a sense of despair as the monotonous routine of prison life takes a toll on his mental health. The prison system is portrayed as dehumanizing, with no consideration for the personal circumstances that led to his crime. Falder’s sense of isolation and despair grows, and he becomes a victim of the rigid and unyielding nature of the justice system.
    • Act IV: The final act sees Falder’s release from prison. However, his life has been irreparably damaged. He finds it difficult to get a job due to his criminal record, and society refuses to accept him. Ruth, who had initially promised to stay by his side, is unable to provide support. In the end, overwhelmed by his circumstances and unable to reintegrate into society, Falder takes his own life. The play ends on a somber note, critiquing the harshness of the justice system and its failure to recognize the humanity of those it punishes.
    • Galsworthy’s play is a powerful critique of the legal and penal systems of the time, emphasizing the need for reform and compassion for those trapped in desperate circumstances.
