BRITISH DRAMA (18TH CENTURY TO MODERN PERIOD)

  • John Galsworthy: Justice

  • written and published in 1910.
    • Characters:-
    • 1. Robert Cokeson: Cokeson is the senior clerk in the law office where much of the play takes place. He is depicted as a kind-hearted, loyal, and morally conservative man, who believes in duty, the law, and authority. Although well-meaning, his sense of justice is limited by his adherence to traditional values, which blinds him to the emotional or moral struggles of others. He tries to help Falder but is constrained by his loyalty to the firm and the legal system.
    • 2. James How: James How is one of the partners in the law firm where Falder works. He is strict, pragmatic, and upholds the rules of the law without much regard for the emotional consequences. He represents the cold, detached side of the legal system, prioritizing professional reputation over personal empathy or compassion.
    • 3. Walter How: Walter How is James How's son and a junior partner in the firm. He is more progressive and sympathetic compared to his father, showing concern for Falder’s situation. Walter offers a contrast to James’s rigid legalism, indicating a newer generation with a more humanitarian approach to justice and morality.
    • 4. Ruth Honeywill: Ruth is the female protagonist of the play. She is a married woman in an abusive relationship with her husband. Her involvement with Falder stems from her desire to escape her oppressive marriage. Ruth’s plight represents the societal constraints on women at the time, and she is portrayed with sensitivity and strength, trying to navigate her circumstances in a harsh world.
    • 5. Falder: Falder is the tragic hero of the play. He is a young clerk working in the law firm and becomes entangled in a moral and legal dilemma when he commits forgery to help Ruth escape her abusive husband. Falder’s actions are driven by compassion and desperation rather than criminal intent. His ultimate downfall and imprisonment are a critique of a rigid legal system that punishes individuals without considering their personal circumstances.
    • 6. Hector Frome: Hector Frome is Falder’s defense attorney. He is portrayed as a compassionate and dedicated lawyer who tries to argue for leniency in Falder’s case. Frome’s approach reflects a belief in reforming the law to accommodate mercy and understanding, contrasting with the harsh and inflexible judicial system of the time.
    • 7. Cleaver: Cleaver is the prosecuting lawyer in the trial against Falder. He is stern, professional, and primarily concerned with enforcing the letter of the law. He represents the impersonal nature of legal prosecution, focusing on Falder’s crime without regard to the broader context or human factors that contributed to it. These characters collectively explore themes of justice, morality, and the shortcomings of the legal system. Galsworthy uses their contrasting perspectives to critique societal and legal norms.
    • SUMMARY:-
    • Summary of John Galsworthy's Play "Justice" (1910)
    • Act I: The play opens in the office of a solicitor, where a young clerk named William Falder is accused of altering a check. Falder is in love with Ruth Honeywill, a married woman in an abusive relationship, and he altered the check to help her escape her husband. The lawyer, Mr. James How, discovers the forgery and decides to press charges. Falder is arrested, despite his emotional plea and his good character.
    • Act II: The second act takes place in the courtroom. Falder’s trial begins, and the defense attorney, Harold Cleaver, tries to convince the jury that Falder’s crime was committed out of desperation to help Ruth. The prosecution, however, emphasizes that altering the check is a serious offense. Ruth's testimony highlights her predicament, but the jury is unsympathetic. Falder is found guilty and sentenced to three years of penal servitude.
    • Act III: The third act takes place in prison. Falder struggles with the harsh conditions of his imprisonment. He feels a sense of despair as the monotonous routine of prison life takes a toll on his mental health. The prison system is portrayed as dehumanizing, with no consideration for the personal circumstances that led to his crime. Falder’s sense of isolation and despair grows, and he becomes a victim of the rigid and unyielding nature of the justice system.
    • Act IV: The final act sees Falder’s release from prison. However, his life has been irreparably damaged. He finds it difficult to get a job due to his criminal record, and society refuses to accept him. Ruth, who had initially promised to stay by his side, is unable to provide support. In the end, overwhelmed by his circumstances and unable to reintegrate into society, Falder takes his own life. The play ends on a somber note, critiquing the harshness of the justice system and its failure to recognize the humanity of those it punishes.
    • Galsworthy’s play is a powerful critique of the legal and penal systems of the time, emphasizing the need for reform and compassion for those trapped in desperate circumstances.
