• T.S. Eliot: Murder in the Cathedral

  • Written and Published - in 1935.
  • Actually happened in - Canterbury,England,December-1170.
  • Cathedral - is a type of Church considered big one.
  • CHARACTERS:-
  • 1. Thomas Becket Role: Archbishop of Canterbury, the protagonist. Characteristics: A man of deep spiritual conviction and faith. Initially conflicted but eventually embraces martyrdom for the sake of the Church. His return to England after a long exile sets the stage for his eventual assassination. He grapples with pride and personal ambition but overcomes these to act as a servant of God. His martyrdom is portrayed as a sacrifice for higher divine justice, which ultimately triumphs over earthly power.
  • 2. The Chorus Role: Representing the women of Canterbury. Characteristics: Serves as the voice of the common people, offering a collective perspective on the events. Reflects the fear and anxiety of ordinary citizens regarding Becket’s return and his impending fate. They are caught between their desire for peace and their dread of the spiritual implications of Becket's martyrdom. They undergo a transformation from fear of change to an understanding of the significance of Becket’s death.
  • 3. The Four Tempters Role: Allegorical figures who tempt Becket to stray from his path. Characteristics: First Tempter: Represents the temptation of physical comfort and pleasure. Offers Becket safety and an easy life if he reconciles with King Henry. Second Tempter: Represents the temptation of power. Suggests that Becket can regain his political influence by aligning with the King. Third Tempter: Represents the temptation of political alliances. Proposes that Becket form an alliance with rebellious barons against the King. Fourth Tempter: The most dangerous temptation, appealing to Becket’s pride. Suggests that martyrdom itself can be a selfish act for personal glory rather than for God's will.
  • 4. The Four Knights Role: Agents of King Henry II, responsible for Becket’s assassination. Characteristics: Serve as the instruments of temporal power, acting on behalf of the King. After Becket’s death, they justify their actions directly to the audience, presenting rational, worldly arguments for the murder. They emphasize that they are acting in the interest of political stability and accuse Becket of betraying the King and the country.
  • 5. The Priests of Canterbury Role: Church officials serving under Becket. Characteristics: Loyal followers of Becket but fearful of the consequences of his actions. They plead with Becket to seek safety and avoid confrontation with the King. Represent the institutional Church, concerned with maintaining order and avoiding conflict. Their fear highlights the tension between worldly concerns and spiritual sacrifice.
  • 6. King Henry II (Offstage) Role: The King of England, whose political conflict with Becket drives the play. Characteristics: Although he never appears on stage, his presence looms large as the antagonist. His desire for control over both the Church and the state leads to a power struggle with Becket. Represents secular authority and the temporal power that Becket challenges through his spiritual convictions. Themes Reflected by Characters: Martyrdom: Becket’s journey reflects the play's focus on the conflict between spiritual integrity and worldly power. Temptation: The Tempters explore different facets of human weakness, mirroring Christ’s temptations in the desert. Political and Religious Conflict: The Knights and King symbolize the tension between secular authority and the autonomy of the Church, a major theme in medieval politics.
  • SUMMARY:-
  • "Murder in the Cathedral" by T.S. Eliot is a verse drama that explores the final days of Thomas Becket, the Archbishop of Canterbury, and his martyrdom in 1170. Here is a summary of the play, paragraph by paragraph:
  • Introduction & Background: The play begins with a chorus of women from Canterbury, who express their fears and anxieties about the return of Archbishop Thomas Becket after seven years of exile. The women sense an impending doom and are uncertain about what Becket's return will bring.
  • Becket’s Return: Becket arrives at Canterbury, greeted by priests who are both happy and anxious. They discuss the political tension between Becket and King Henry II, which had led to Becket's exile. Becket reflects on his role as a servant of God and the conflicts between spiritual authority and temporal power.
  • The Tempters: Becket is confronted by four tempters who represent various forms of power and pleasure.
    The first tempter offers physical safety and comfort if Becket reconciles with the king.
    The second offers power and influence in the state.
    The third suggests the possibility of leading a rebellion against the king.
    The fourth tempter, more subtly, tempts Becket with the idea of martyrdom for the sake of glory.
    Becket rejects all the temptations, recognizing the last one as the most dangerous because it disguises selfish pride as piety.
  • Sermon: Becket delivers a Christmas sermon to the congregation. He speaks about the nature of martyrdom and the idea that true martyrdom is not sought for personal glory but is a submission to the will of God. He reflects on the mystery of Christ’s birth and the inevitability of his own death.
  • The Knights: Four knights arrive, representing King Henry II. They accuse Becket of betrayal and treason against the crown. Becket, unwavering in his stance, refuses to submit to the king’s authority in matters of the church. The knights leave but later return to confront Becket in the cathedral.
  • The Martyrdom: The chorus again expresses their fears and laments the fate of their archbishop. The knights return and murder Becket in the cathedral. After the murder, the knights justify their actions to the audience, claiming they acted out of duty to the king and the state, not out of personal malice.
  • Conclusion & Reflection: The play concludes with the chorus reflecting on the significance of Becket's martyrdom. They recognize the event as both a tragedy and a divine act, acknowledging that Becket's sacrifice has a profound impact on the spiritual life of the community and the church.
  • The play explores deep themes such as the conflict between church and state, the nature of power and authority, and the meaning of martyrdom. Eliot uses poetic language and the chorus to emphasize the spiritual and existential questions that the play raises.
