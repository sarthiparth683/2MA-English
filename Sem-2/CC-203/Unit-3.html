  • SUMMARY:-
      • "Murder in the Cathedral" by T.S. Eliot is a play written in verse that dramatizes the assassination of Archbishop Thomas Becket in Canterbury Cathedral in 1170. The play is structured in two acts, with an interlude between them. Here's an act-wise summary:
  • Act I: Setting & Background: The play begins in December 1170, in the Archbishop's Hall at Canterbury Cathedral. The Chorus, consisting of women of Canterbury, introduces the atmosphere of unease and foreboding. They express their fear of an impending disaster and reflect on the Archbishop's absence from England for seven years. Thomas Becket's Return: Becket returns from exile in France, having had a conflict with King Henry II over the rights and privileges of the Church. Upon his return, four Tempters approach him, each offering different paths to avoid conflict with the King and preserve his life: First Tempter: Offers physical safety and the pleasures of life. Second Tempter: Suggests political power by joining forces with the King. Third Tempter: Proposes a rebellion against the King in alliance with the barons. Fourth Tempter: Appeals to Becket's spiritual pride, suggesting he seek martyrdom for personal glory. Becket's Response: Becket rejects all the temptations, especially the fourth one, recognizing that seeking martyrdom for his own glory would be a sin. He decides to follow the path of righteousness, accepting whatever fate God has in store for him. Interlude: Becket's Sermon: The interlude consists of a sermon delivered by Becket on Christmas morning. He speaks about the meaning of martyrdom, emphasizing that a martyr is someone who is killed for the sake of God and not for personal gain. He also prepares the people for his possible death, indicating that it may be a part of God's will.
  • Act II: Becket's Resolve: The second act begins after Christmas, with the Chorus again expressing their fear and sense of impending doom. Four knights, who serve King Henry II, arrive at the Cathedral. They accuse Becket of betraying the King and the country, and they demand that he submit to the King’s authority. The Knights' Accusation: The knights accuse Becket of treason and sacrilege. Becket, standing firm in his convictions, refuses to submit to the King's demands, asserting the supremacy of the Church over the state. Becket’s Martyrdom: The knights, enraged by Becket's defiance, murder him in the Cathedral. The Chorus laments the Archbishop’s death, feeling guilt for not having done more to prevent it but also recognizing the sanctity of his sacrifice.
  • Conclusion: The knights justify their actions to the audience, claiming they acted in defense of the King and the nation. However, the play ends with a sense of divine judgment, as the Chorus prays for God's mercy and for the Archbishop's soul. The play closes on a note of ambiguity, leaving the audience to reflect on the complex interplay of politics, religion, and morality.
  • Themes: Conflict between Church and State: The play highlights the tension between secular authority and spiritual power. Martyrdom and Sacrifice: It explores the concept of martyrdom, questioning the motivations and implications of seeking such a fate. Temptation and Moral Integrity: The tempters represent various forms of temptation, and Becket’s resistance to them illustrates the struggle to maintain moral integrity. "Murder in the Cathedral" is a powerful exploration of faith, power, and the human condition, written with poetic depth and dramatic intensity.
