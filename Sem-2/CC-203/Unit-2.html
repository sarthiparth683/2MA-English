• G.B. Shaw: Pygmalion

  • Characters:-
  • Eliza Doolittle Role: The protagonist, a poor flower girl with a strong Cockney accent. Characteristics: Eliza is ambitious and eager to improve her station in life. She is tough, independent, and determined but vulnerable due to her social position. Her transformation from a common street vendor to a lady capable of speaking "proper" English symbolizes the central theme of social mobility and identity in the play.
  • Professor Henry Higgins Role: A professor of phonetics and the play's other main protagonist. Characteristics: Higgins is a brilliant but arrogant and socially indifferent man. He is passionate about phonetics and sees people more as subjects for his experiments rather than as human beings. His cold and emotionally distant nature contrasts with his keen intellect, and though he helps transform Eliza's life, he often disregards her feelings.
  • Colonel Pickering Role: A retired officer and expert in phonetics, Higgins' friend and colleague. Characteristics: Pickering is kind, courteous, and gentlemanly, providing a foil to Higgins' brusque and sometimes thoughtless behavior. His respectful treatment of Eliza helps her gain confidence and is a key part of her transformation. He represents the moral and humanistic side of the phonetics experiment.
  • Alfred Doolittle Role: Eliza's father, a dustman (garbage collector). Characteristics: Doolittle is a cheerful, opportunistic, and philosophically cynical man. He has no desire for middle-class morality or responsibility but unexpectedly inherits a fortune that thrusts him into the very world he wanted to avoid. His speeches often satirize societal hypocrisy and the nature of class and morality.
  • Mrs. Higgins Role: Henry Higgins' mother. Characteristics: Mrs. Higgins is wise, poised, and has a strong moral compass. She disapproves of her son's callousness and lack of social graces, often acting as a voice of reason. She has a better understanding of Eliza's emotional journey and shows genuine concern for her well-being.
  • Freddy Eynsford-Hill Role: A young man from an impoverished aristocratic family, smitten with Eliza. Characteristics: Freddy is charming, idealistic, and somewhat naive. Though he comes from a higher class than Eliza, his family has lost their wealth. He falls in love with Eliza and contrasts with Higgins in his gentle, romantic pursuit of her.
  • Mrs. Eynsford-Hill Role: Freddy's mother. Characteristics: Mrs. Eynsford-Hill is a well-meaning but financially struggling woman. She clings to the pretense of gentility, despite her family's declining circumstances. She represents the middle class, which is under strain during the societal changes of the time.
  • Mrs. Pearce Role: Higgins' housekeeper. Characteristics: Mrs. Pearce is practical, no-nonsense, and motherly. She plays a significant role in managing Higgins' household and often warns him about treating Eliza poorly. She serves as a voice of morality, showing concern for Eliza’s future and well-being.
  • Ezra Wannafeller (not a major character in the play but often mentioned in modern references) Role: A fictional character alluded to in some adaptations as a wealthy philanthropist. Characteristics: Typically depicted as a wealthy industrialist, his name is a parody of American magnates like Rockefeller. He is used to represent the influence of wealth and charity in transforming people’s lives, though sometimes with unintended consequences.
  • SUMMARY:-
  • "Pygmalion" by George Bernard Shaw is a play that explores themes of social class, identity, and transformation. Here's an act-wise summary:
  • Act I The play opens in Covent Garden, London, where a group of people seek shelter from the rain. Among them are Professor Henry Higgins, a phonetician, and Colonel Pickering, another expert in phonetics. Higgins impresses the crowd by identifying everyone's place of origin based on their accents. They notice a poor flower girl, Eliza Doolittle, who speaks with a strong Cockney accent. Higgins boasts that he could teach her to speak like a duchess in six months. After some interaction, Higgins and Pickering decide to work together on the project.
  • Act II The next day, Eliza visits Higgins' laboratory to take him up on his offer to improve her speech, hoping it will help her get a job in a flower shop. Higgins agrees, partly out of scientific curiosity, and Pickering wagers the cost of the experiment. They begin a rigorous process of teaching Eliza to speak properly, which includes correcting her pronunciation, teaching her manners, and changing her appearance. Meanwhile, Eliza's father, Alfred Doolittle, visits Higgins, hoping to get some money in exchange for his daughter's involvement. After some negotiation, he leaves with five pounds.
  • Act III After months of training, Higgins decides to test Eliza at his mother's at-home party. Eliza successfully passes herself off as a refined lady, fooling everyone with her new speech and manners. However, her conversation is somewhat limited and repetitive. Later, Higgins and Pickering take her to an ambassador's garden party, where she successfully passes as a duchess, and even fools a former pupil of Higgins. Despite the success, Eliza begins to feel unsure of her place in the world, questioning her identity now that she is neither part of the lower class nor truly accepted by the upper class.
  • Act IV Following the successful experiment, Higgins and Pickering celebrate their achievement, seemingly oblivious to Eliza’s feelings. Eliza feels dehumanized and treated like an object. A confrontation ensues between Eliza and Higgins, where she throws his slippers at him in anger. She tells Higgins that she plans to leave and marry Freddy Eynsford-Hill, a young man from an earlier act who has fallen in love with her. Higgins tries to dismiss her concerns, but Eliza is determined to assert her independence.
  • Act V The final act is set at Mrs. Higgins' house. Eliza arrives there, and Alfred Doolittle, now unexpectedly wealthy due to an inheritance, also arrives. He bemoans his new responsibilities but is on his way to marry his long-time partner. Higgins arrives and tries to convince Eliza to return to his house, but she refuses. They argue about her future, and while it is clear that Higgins cares for Eliza in his way, he cannot express it. Eliza asserts her independence and leaves with Freddy. The play ends ambiguously, leaving it unclear whether Eliza will return to Higgins or choose a life with Freddy.
